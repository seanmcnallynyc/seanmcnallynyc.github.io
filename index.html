Context

In our AI-assisted collaborative songwriting protocol for advanced cancer patients, generative music software gave patients with no musical background real authorship over their songs, and reduced therapist production workload outside of sessions.

What I saw, delivering these sessions myself

I watched the same moment happen over and over: a patient would arrive with a clear feeling, sit down in front of the AI tool’s blank prompt field, and freeze. The gap was between what a patient was feeling and what the interface needed to hear, and for some patients that gap ate the entire hour.

Getting buy-in

This wasn’t a solo build shipped into a vacuum. I brought the problem to regular team meetings with the study PI, the co-therapists running their own sessions who’d hit the same wall, and an outside music therapy research advisor. Each revision was shaped by their pushback and tested in mock sessions with co-therapists, interns, med students, or post-docs before I moved on.

What I built

Suno is a standalone product — we have a relationship with the company, but no access to their interface. So rather than wait on their roadmap, I built the scaffold to sit outside it entirely. It was the second tool I’d built against the same underlying problem: the emotion wheel helps someone name a feeling; the scaffold turns that into language a generative model can act on. Try the live tool ↗ · View the prompt logic ↗

“The Lyric Scaffold removes the burden of prompt-engineering so I can focus on the patient’s needs.” Therapist feedback, early use

Outcome

Not yet formally evaluated. The scaffold is in active use ahead of VIRTUOSO, where testing time-to-draft and completion rate against the prior protocol is the next planned analysis.

Patient feedback

“That tool for designing prompts is really a good thing — it translates basic feelings/needs of patients to the interface of Suno.”Patient, outpatient setting

This is the only direct patient feedback collected so far — the outpatient context allowed it, but that’s not always an option in a stricter clinical trial environment. The next step is a formal patient feedback protocol within the IRB-approved research process.

What I’d do differently

Every round of iteration ran through clinicians first, the study PI, co-therapists, and our research advisor, before it reached a patient. That’s a reasonable way to move fast inside a clinical protocol, but it means the scaffold was validated by the people delivering it before it was tested directly with the people using it. If I rebuilt this, I’d put a patient in the design loop earlier instead of inferring their experience secondhand through a therapist’s report of it.

Studies

Research programs I work on

MELODY

Is telehealth-delivered music therapy as effective as CBT for anxiety in cancer survivors?

Published Noninferior to CBT at both 8 and 26 weeks — durable improvement in both arms

Role: screening, eligibility, and IRB-approved consent across a 300-participant multi-site trial, with HIPAA-compliant handling of PHI, audio, and survey data, plus therapy delivery and protocol refinement. Collected validated patient-reported outcomes (HADS, Cancer Loneliness Scale, Impact of Events Scale, FACT-Cog, an insomnia scale, and others, full list in the published paper), including calling participants directly to collect responses by phone when they hadn’t completed a survey on their own, to keep the dataset complete. Present ongoing study updates to stakeholders, including patient advocates and funders such as the Patient-Centered Outcomes Research Institute (PCORI). Not an author on the published outcomes.

Liou et al., J Clin Oncol 44:375–385, 2026. DOI 10.1200/JCO-25-00726 ↗ · a qualitative companion paper has been accepted for publication and is forthcoming.
